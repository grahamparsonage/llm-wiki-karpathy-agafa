UK-focused Forensic Science Regulator draft guidance describing a full method-validation lifecycle for digital forensics, centered on end-user requirements, risk-based test design, and documented evidence of fitness for purpose.

## Citation
Forensic Science Regulator. (2015). *Draft Guidance: Digital Forensics Method Validation (FSR-G-218), Second consultation draft*. Crown copyright.

## Key points
- Defines validation as objective evidence that a method, process, or device is fit for a specific purpose, and ties this directly to court reliability expectations and ISO/IEC 17025 compliance.
- Emphasizes that validation target is the end-to-end method (including practitioner interaction), not isolated tools.
- Requires explicit end-user requirements before test planning; these requirements drive acceptance criteria, data design, and interpretation of limitations.
- Frames risk assessment as core to validation scope, including risks of wrongful conviction, wrongful acquittal, and investigation delay.
- Distinguishes validation effort for novel methods, adopted/adapted methods, and minor changes, with explicit conditions for verification versus re-validation.
- Requires strategy/plan documentation, controlled representative test data, contemporaneous records, and comparison of observed versus expected outcomes.
- Requires formal closure artifacts: validation report plus statement/certificate of validation completion, followed by implementation controls (training, QA, documentation upkeep).
- Highlights uncertainty, competency, and reporting caveats as part of method validity, not optional afterthoughts.

## Scope note
This source is a 2015 consultation draft and should not be treated as a published standard; where draft guidance conflicts with binding requirements, the Codes and ISO/IEC 17025 prevail.
