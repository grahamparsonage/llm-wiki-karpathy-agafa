Perspective paper framing how explainable AI could move digital forensics from opaque classifier-assisted triage toward more transparent, user-centric investigative support and reporting.

## Citation
Hall, S. W., Sakzad, A., & Choo, K.-K. R. (2022). *Explainable artificial intelligence for digital forensics*. Wiley Interdisciplinary Reviews: Forensic Science, 4(2), e1434. https://doi.org/10.1002/wfs2.1434

## Key points
- Distinguishes four XAI categories (opaque, interpretable, comprehensible, truly explainable) and argues DF use in courts demands more than opaque tagging/classification.
- Notes current commercial DF AI tooling is largely opaque and examiner-validated, useful for discovery/triage but not self-sufficient for interpretation or courtroom explanation.
- Emphasizes user-centric explainability: outputs must be understandable by mixed audiences (investigators, examiners, legal actors), not only technically valid.
- Proposes a "human-on-the-loop" transition rather than full human removal, with automated support focused on prioritization, case management, and report acceleration.
- Highlights operational constraints for adoption: legally controlled training data, practitioner AI/ML competence needs, and differing public/private sector transparency requirements.
- Introduces an "XAI for Investigative Support" workflow concept (scoping, data collection, model processing, referenced automated reporting) as a forward model.
- Flags adversarial ML, biased/tainted training data, and deepfake robustness as trust risks for DF AI deployment.

## Scope note
This paper is a perspective/opinion-style agenda-setting source, not a validation standard. It is strongest as a conceptual and operational direction-setting input for DF AI design and governance.
