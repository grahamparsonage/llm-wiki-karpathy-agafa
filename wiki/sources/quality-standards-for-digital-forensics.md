Empirical review of quality standard implementation in England and Wales digital forensics, showing where accreditation improves reliability and where implementation burden remains high.

## Citation
Tully, G., Cohen, N., Compton, D., Davies, G., Isbell, R., & Watson, T. (2020). *Quality standards for digital forensics: Learning from experience in England & Wales*. Forensic Science International: Digital Investigation, 32, 200905.

## Key points
- Examines implementation of ISO/IEC 17025 and the Forensic Science Regulator's Codes in digital forensics services in England and Wales.
- Uses data from UKAS assessments (initial and surveillance) plus quality referrals to the Regulator, rather than purely theoretical debate.
- Finds high rates of early non-conformance in procedures, validation, records, competency evidence, and ongoing quality assurance.
- Reports measurable improvement after accreditation implementation, with fewer findings at surveillance than at initial assessments.
- Shows quality standards are not a guarantee of perfect outcomes, but provide structured external assurance and systematic improvement pressure.
- Highlights recurring implementation stressors: validation burden, inconsistent procedures, weak technical records, and exhibit-handling continuity issues.
- Recommends shared infrastructure to reduce duplication (transparent specifications, ground-truth datasets, central tool testing resources, learning sharing).

## Scope note
This source is UK-focused and reflects England/Wales regulatory and accreditation practice (for example UKAS and the Forensic Science Regulator); direct transfer to other jurisdictions requires adaptation.
