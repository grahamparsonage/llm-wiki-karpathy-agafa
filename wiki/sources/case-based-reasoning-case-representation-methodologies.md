Survey paper reviewing case-representation methods in case-based reasoning (CBR), with emphasis on ontology-driven (knowledge-intensive) approaches and their limitations.

## Citation

El-Sappagh, S. H., & Elmogy, M. (2015). *Case Based Reasoning: Case Representation Methodologies*. International Journal of Advanced Computer Science and Applications, 6(11), 192-208.

## Problem and aim

Case representation is a core design choice in CBR and strongly affects retrieval quality, adaptation potential, and maintainability. The paper surveys traditional and semantic (ontology-centric) representation strategies, compares ontological CBR methods, and identifies open challenges for future systems.

## Method snapshot

- Broad literature search over CBR representation work (roughly 1990-2012).
- Organizes methods into traditional forms (feature vector, frames, objects, textual, hierarchical, predicate) and semantic/ontological forms.
- Compares ontology-based CBR frameworks using a multi-criteria checklist (for example retrieval semantics, adaptability, heterogeneity handling, maintenance support).

## Key findings

- Ontology-enriched CBR generally improves semantic retrieval and interoperability versus purely syntactic representations.
- Existing ontology-based CBR frameworks remained incomplete against the authors' criteria, indicating practical maturity gaps.
- Case-base update/maintenance, explanation support, and robust handling of heterogeneous case structures were highlighted as recurring weak points.
- Integration of rules with ontology models can improve retrieval and adaptation, but creates design and governance complexity.

## Relevance to this wiki

- Strengthens the conceptual basis for ontology-plus-rules approaches already central to AGAFA framing.
- Reinforces that retrieval quality depends on representation design, not only model choice.
- Adds historical context for current interest in structured, explainable, and maintainable knowledge representations.
